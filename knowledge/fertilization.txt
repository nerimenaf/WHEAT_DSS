Source: Wheat Fertilization Guidelines (General + Precision Nitrogen)

==============================
SECTION 1 – GENERAL PRINCIPLES
==============================

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p1]
Fertilizer recommendations for wheat depend on soil fertility, previous crop, soil texture, irrigation availability, rainfall, yield potential, and management level.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p2]
Lower fertilizer rates are recommended for fertile soils. Higher rates are recommended for poor soils.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p3]
Nitrogen (N) is the most limiting nutrient for high-yielding wheat production.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p4]
Nitrogen availability during the growing season strongly influences grain protein content and grain quality.

==============================
SECTION 2 – RECOMMENDED RATES
==============================

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p5]
Wheat (Irrigated – Punjab I, II, III):
N: 75–160 kg/ha
P2O5: 60–110 kg/ha
K2O: 60 kg/ha

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p6]
Wheat (Rainfed – Punjab V):
N: 100 kg/ha
P2O5: 75 kg/ha
K2O: 60 kg/ha

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p7]
Wheat (Rainfed – NWFP I):
N: 60–100 kg/ha
P2O5: 30–60 kg/ha

==============================
SECTION 3 – APPLICATION TIMING
==============================

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p8]
All phosphorus and potassium and half of the nitrogen should be incorporated into the soil before sowing.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p9]
If phosphorus is not applied at sowing, it can be applied at the first irrigation.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p10]
The remaining half of nitrogen should be top-dressed at the first or second irrigation.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p11]
On light-textured soils, nitrogen should be applied in three split doses to reduce losses.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p12]
In rainfed areas, the full fertilizer dose may be applied at sowing.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p13]
For late planting, apply all fertilizers at sowing and slightly increase the total dose.

==============================
SECTION 4 – PRECISION NITROGEN MANAGEMENT
==============================

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p14]
Improper nitrogen application can lead to nitrogen losses before plant uptake, reducing efficiency.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p15]
Optimal nitrogen management requires balancing sufficient nitrogen supply with minimizing environmental losses.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p16]
Satellite-based vegetation indices can estimate wheat nitrogen uptake and guide in-season nitrogen recommendations.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p17]
Handheld crop sensors can measure plant nitrogen concentration during key growth stages to support nitrogen decisions.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p18]
Variable Rate Application (VRA) allows nitrogen fertilizer rates to be adjusted within a field based on crop nutrient demand.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p19]
In arid and semi-arid regions, nitrogen use efficiency is strongly influenced by soil moisture availability.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p20]
Insufficient soil moisture can limit nitrogen uptake even when fertilizer is applied at adequate rates.

[fertilization_wheat_full.txt::p21]
Well-adjusted nitrogen rates improve fertilizer use efficiency, increase yield and grain quality, and reduce environmental risk.
